1 Introduction 

Painting is a sequential creative process. Unlike constructing images by optimizing pixels toward a predefined target, artists progres-sively develop compositions through a series of context-dependent brushstroke decisions. Each stroke reflects semantic understanding, spatial reasoning, style preference, local visual context, and prior painting history. Artistic intent evolves dynamically throughout the process: artists refine compositions, react to emerging structures, and continuously reinterpret the canvas as the work develops. As a result, painting is open-ended and temporally evolving rather than a static image reconstruction task. Early work in inverse graphics and procedural image model-ing established the foundation for representing images through structured graphical programs, differentiable rendering, and se-quential stroke representations[ 12 , 15 , 17 , 23 ]. Subsequent neural painting systems extended these ideas through reinforcement learn-ing, differentiable neural rendering, and transformer-based stroke generation, enabling agents to synthesize images through proce-dural brushstrokes[ 4, 10 , 11 , 14 , 32 ]. These systems progressively advanced neural painting from low-level stroke optimization toward more semantically aware and compositionally structured painting generation[28]. Despite substantial progress, existing neural painting methods re-main fundamentally target-driven [ 7 , 8]. Specifically, the final output is explicitly specified by a reference target image, and brushstroke generation becomes a constrained optimization problem toward pixel reconstruction [ 4, 10 , 14 ]. As a result, these methods primar-ily learn how to reproduce images rather than how to continue paintings from evolving canvas states and prior artistic actions. In particular, they do not explicitly model stylistic continuation, tempo-rally coherent brushstroke evolution, or painting generation beyond predefined reconstruction objectives [24, 27]. To address these limitations, we introduce PaintCopilot , a co-creative neural painting assistant that learns and continues an artist’s brushstroke behavior without requiring a target image, as illustrated in Figure 1. Instead of framing painting as image recon-struction, we model painting as an autoregressive artistic contin-uation problem [ 6, 26 ]: given the current canvas state and prior brushstroke history, the system predicts future strokes directly from learned artistic dynamics, analogous to how large language mod-els [ 18 ] continue text sequences from prior context. In practice, artists paint freely and hand control to PaintCopilot, which au-tonomously continues the artwork in their learned style. The paper makes the following contributions: 1) We introduce autonomous painting continuation , a new neural painting formulation that removes explicit target-image con-ditioning and models painting as an open-ended sequential artistic behavior conditioned on evolving canvas states and prior brush-stroke history. 2) We propose three complementary models: a ViT-based Target Predictor that infers artist intent from partial canvas observations, an autoregressive Next Stroke Predictor that generates temporally coherent brushstrokes via flow matching, and a VAE-based Region Sampler that synthesizes semantically localized stroke sequences on demand. 3) We develop an interactive human-AI painting system with three differentiable brush representations (Hard Round, Brush Tip, and 2D Gaussian), supporting autonomous continuation, history optimization, dynamic brush refinement, and region-aware inpaint-ing, enabling seamless collaborative painting workflows between artists and AI. 

2 Related Work 2.1 Inverse Graphics and Stroke-Based Representation 

Early work in inverse graphics investigated how to recover struc-tured procedural representations from images rather than directly operating on pixels [ 12 , 17 ]. These approaches modeled images through latent programs, geometric primitives, and differentiable rendering processes, establishing foundational paradigms for proce-dural visual generation. Subsequent work such as OpenDR intro-duced differentiable rendering frameworks for optimization over graphical parameters [ 15 ], while CSG-based representations ex-plored executable geometric decompositions of images [ 23 ]. In par-allel, sequential stroke representations emerged through autoregres-sive drawing and sketch generation models such as SketchRNN[ 5], demonstrating that free-form drawing trajectories could be modeled as temporally ordered stroke sequences. These approaches estab-lished important foundations for procedural stroke generation and differentiable brush parameterization, which later inspired neural painting systems. 

2.2 Target-Driven Neural Painting 

A major line of work formulates neural painting as reconstructing a predefined target image through sequential brushstroke generation. Reinforcement learning approaches such as SPIRAL [ 4] and Learn-ing to Paint [ 10 ] formulate painting as a sequential decision-making problem, where an agent iteratively places strokes to maximize sim-ilarity to a target image. Follow-up works including IntelliPaint[ 24 ], HRL-Painter[ 30 ], and attention-guided painting systems[ 9] further improve semantic planning and stroke efficiency within target-conditioned environments. Another direction focuses on optimization-based neural painting through differentiable rendering. Stylized Neural Painting [ 32 ] and parameterized brushstroke style transfer methods [ 11 ] optimize brushstroke parameters through differentiable rendering pipelines and progressive refinement strategies. These methods typically op-timize all strokes jointly through test-time optimization rather than modeling a temporally evolving painting process.

More recent transformer-based systems such as Paint Transformer[ 14 ]and Compositional Neural Painter[ 7] improve semantic stroke plan-ning through autoregressive or compositional generation strate-gies. However, despite architectural differences, these methods re-main fundamentally target-driven: brushstrokes are generated to reproduce a reference image rather than to model the open-ended decision-making behavior of human artists. 

2.3 Interactive and Artist-Like Painting 

Beyond fully automatic image reconstruction, recent work has ex-plored more artist-like and interactive painting systems that empha-size semantic planning, interpretable painting behavior, and human-AI collaboration. IntelliPaint [ 24 ] investigates human-intelligible painting agents that produce artist-like stroke sequences, while re-cent semantic alignment approaches incorporate multi-granularity semantic guidance and compositional reasoning to improve artist-like painting generation [ 8]. ProcessPainter [ 25 ] learns to model the painting process as a sequential progression from sparse to dense stroke coverage, demonstrating that painting order and temporal structure carry meaningful information beyond the final image. Inverse Painting [ 22 ] reconstructs plausible stroke-level painting processes from finished artworks using a video diffusion model, showing that temporal painting dynamics can be recovered from static images alone. While these approaches move neural painting closer to process-aware and stylistically coherent generation, they either require a finished target image as input or model painting as a video gen-eration problem rather than an interactive continuation task. In contrast, PaintCopilot focuses on autonomous painting continu-ation from evolving canvas states and prior brushstroke history, enabling co-creative human-AI painting workflows without requir-ing a reference target image. 

3 Preliminaries 3.1 Human Painting Behavior Analysis 

Unlike target-driven neural painting systems that optimize strokes toward reconstructing a predefined image, we model painting as a temporally evolving human artistic behavior. Real painters continu-ously adjust brush size, pressure, stroke rhythm, and color usage throughout the painting process. To better understand these dy-namics and motivate our autoregressive formulation, we analyze human brushstroke trajectories collected from real digital painting sessions. We develop a custom painting interface that continuously records real-time brush interactions during free-form painting. Each stroke contains brush metadata including tool type, brush size, color, smooth-ing parameters, and a temporally ordered sequence of tablet samples storing 2D position, pressure, and relative timestamp information. Human artists freely paint within the system, producing realistic painter demonstrations rather than procedurally generated strokes. Sessions generated using mouse input are automatically filtered by detecting degenerate constant-pressure trajectories. Figure 2 presents statistical analysis of human painting behavior. We observe strong multi-scale brush dynamics throughout paint-ing progression, where large brushes dominate early compositional stages while smaller brushes increasingly appear during detail re-finement. Pressure trajectories remain smooth and continuously varying, indicating dynamic control of opacity and texture accumula-tion during stroke execution. Stroke lengths exhibit heavy-tailed dis-tributions, with long structural strokes appearing more frequently during early layout construction and shorter strokes dominating later refinement stages. Color usage evolves throughout painting progression, revealing temporally organized palette behavior. We curate a dataset of 3,000 classical portrait paintings (see Fig-ure 4), each paired with a stroke sequence of 300–400 strokes that simulates human painting order, as well as the intermediate canvas state after every stroke. • Segmentation. Two complementary models provide semantic masks at multiple levels of granularity. BiSeNet [ 29 ] segments large-scale regions including background, hair, clothing, hat, neck, and ears; MediaPipe Face Mesh [ 16 ] provides precise facial sub-regions by rasterizing landmark mesh polygons for the face, eyes, nose, mouth, and their sub-structures. • Normal Map. Per-pixel surface normals are estimated using DSINE [ 2]. Normal maps directly encode light-to-shadow transitions central to oil painting techniques like Chiaroscuro, motivating their use for ordering strokes by local normal vari-ance over an 8×8 pixel neighborhood: flat areas are painted first with large strokes and geometrically complex areas last with smaller, more precise strokes. 

• Attention Map. The self-attention of DINO [ 3 ] has been shown to correlate with human visual saliency [ 1 ]. We use the last-layer center-patch attention from a ViT-S/16 model as a spa-tial sampling weight, concentrating strokes on perceptually salient regions and sparser elsewhere. The three signals are combined into a unified algorithm (Figure 5). Regions are processed in coarse-to-fine label order. Within each region, 𝑁 stroke positions are sampled from the DINO attention map and scored by score 𝑖 = 100 · ˆ𝜎 𝑖 + ˆ𝑎 𝑖 , sorting flat, less-salient positions first. Brush sizes follow a linear decay from a region-specific maximum to minimum. Once assembled, stroke parameters are jointly optimized against the target image via differentiable rendering and MSE loss using Adam with cosine annealing over 30 iterations with early stopping. 

4.3 Model Architecture 

4.3.1 Target Predictor. The Target Predictor dynamically estimates the artist’s intended outcome from the current canvas state alone, analogous to how a human painter draws from imagination. Con-cretely, all intermediate canvas states across the painting progres-sion share a single ground truth target, forming a many-to-one supervised prediction task. The model operates in the latent space of a pretrained Stable Diffusion VAE [21]. The canvas latent is patchified into a sequence of tokens with fixed 2D sinusoidal positional embeddings and processed by a lightweight Vision Transformer. Rather than directly predicting the target latent, the model predicts a residual Δ𝑧 added back to the canvas latent to produce the estimate ˆ𝑧 = 𝑧 𝑐 + Δ𝑧 . This residual formulation biases the model toward plausible completions close to the current canvas state, stabilizing training. The model is supervised with a combined L1 and MSE loss in VAE latent space:

4.3.3 Region Sampler. The Region Sampler generates a complete stroke sequence for a given semantic region directly from a sampled latent code, without requiring a canvas state or prior stroke history. This makes it complementary to the Next Stroke Predictor, which requires existing stroke context, enabling cold-start generation for empty or user-specified regions. The model is a Variational Autoencoder operating on sequences of stroke parameters. The encoder prepends a learnable CLS token to the stroke sequence, processes it with a Transformer, and projects the CLS output to a Gaussian posterior 𝑞 (𝑧 | 𝑥 ) = N ( 𝜇, 𝜎 2) via separate linear heads. The decoder conditions on both the sampled latent 𝑧 and a semantic label embedding, broadcasts the combined representation to a fixed-length token sequence, and reconstructs stroke parameters through a Transformer followed by a sigmoid output projection. Stroke positions are represented in local bounding box coordinates normalized to the region extent, making the latent space invariant to region location and scale on the canvas.

4.4 Training Details 

All models are trained on 8 NVIDIA RTX Pro 6000 GPUs using AdamW with gradient clipping at 1.0 and cosine annealing learning rate schedules with linear warmup. The Target Predictor and Next Stroke Predictor are each trained for 500 epochs with a learning rate of 10 −4 and batch sizes of 256 and 512 respectively. The Region Sampler is trained for 5,000 epochs with a learning rate of 10 −3 and batch size 128, with 𝛽 annealed linearly from 10 −4 to 10 −3 over the first 2,000 epochs to stabilize VAE training. All models are trained on the same dataset of 3,000 portrait paintings described in Section 4.2. 

4.5 System Implementation 

The system is implemented in Python using PyQt6 and runs in real-time on a single consumer GPU, with all optimization and in-ference performed on CUDA. The interactive PaintCopilot interface is shown in Figure 7. 

4.5.1 Optimize History. Optimize History refines the artist’s ex-isting brushstrokes by re-optimizing their parameters toward the Target Predictor’s estimated intent. The recorded stroke history is converted into differentiable brush parameters, and the Target Predictor generates a predicted target image from the current can-vas state. Stroke parameters including position, color, size, angle, and pressure are then jointly optimized via differentiable rendering to minimize MSE loss against the predicted target. The optimized strokes are re-rendered onto the canvas, improving global coherence without requiring any manual input from the artist. 

4.5.2 Stroke Completion. Stroke Completion enables the artist to hand control to the AI, which autonomously continues the painting conditioned on the artist’s prior stroke history and canvas state. The current canvas is encoded into a VAE latent and passed to the Tar-get Predictor to estimate the intended final image. The Next Stroke Predictor then samples the next stroke conditioned on the canvas state and the prior stroke history. The predicted stroke is further refined via differentiable optimization toward the predicted target before being committed to the canvas. This process repeats itera-tively, with the canvas and stroke context updated after each step, until the artist resumes control. If a lasso mask is active, predicted stroke positions are constrained to the selected region. 

4.5.3 Region Inpainting. Region Inpainting populates a user-specified region with a complete stroke sequence generated by the Region Sampler. The artist draws a lasso mask to define the target region, then selects a semantic label corresponding to the desired content. The model samples a latent code and decodes a sequence of strokes localized to the bounding box of the selected region, with stroke positions expressed in local coordinates relative to the region extent. The generated strokes are immediately rendered onto the canvas, allowing the artist to populate large regions with semantically co-herent brushwork in a single interaction. 

4.5.4 Dynamic Brush. Dynamic Brush optimizes each individual stroke locally as soon as the artist lifts the stylus. After each stroke, a crop of the canvas surrounding the stroke’s bounding box is ex-tracted, and the stroke parameters are refined via differentiable rendering against the Target Predictor’s output for that region. 5 Evaluation 5.1 Target Predictor Evaluation 

We evaluate the Target Predictor on 100 held-out paintings sampled from outside the training set, measuring prediction quality against the ground truth target image at ten evenly spaced stages of painting progression. We report LPIPS [ 31 ], SSIM, and PSNR between the predicted and ground truth target images decoded from VAE latent space. Figure 9 shows that prediction quality improves consistently as the painting progresses. At the blank canvas stage, LPIPS is 0.89, SSIM is 0.25, and PSNR is 4.88 dB, reflecting the difficulty of inferring intent from an empty canvas. After just 11% of strokes are laid down, LPIPS drops sharply to 0.48 and PSNR rises to 17.40 dB, indicating that early compositional strokes provide sufficient semantic context for a coarse prediction. Prediction quality continues to improve steadily through completion, reaching LPIPS of 0.39, SSIM of 0.38, and PSNR of 19.44 dB at 100% progression. The overall mean across all stages is LPIPS = 0.47 , SSIM = 0.36 , PSNR = 17 .43 dB. 5.2 Autoregressive Stroke Predictor Evaluation 

We evaluate the Autoregressive Stroke Predictor on 100 held-out paintings sampled from outside the training set. Given a window of 50 prior strokes and the current canvas state, the model predicts the next stroke as an 8-dimensional parameter vector. We measure mean L1 distance between predicted and ground truth stroke parameters, reported both overall and per dimension. The overall mean L1 distance across all dimensions is 0.100. Per-dimension analysis (Figure 10) reveals that color is the hardest dimension to predict, with L1 errors of 0.235, 0.150, and 0.089 for R, G, and B respectively, reflecting the inherently personal and context-dependent nature of color choices in artistic practice. Brush size is the most predictable dimension (L1 = 0.011 ), consistent with the strong coarse-to-fine structure imposed during dataset construction. Spatial position, pressure, and angle fall in between, with L1 errors of 0.094, 0.060, 0.099, and 0.061 respectively. Figure 11 shows prediction error across painting progression. Error peaks at 35% progression (L1 = 0.137 ), corresponding to the transitional phase where coarse structure has been established but fine detail refinement has not yet begun, making individual stroke decisions most ambiguous. Error decreases steadily in the latter half of the painting as strokes become more locally constrained by surrounding detail, reaching 0.070 at 85% progression.

6 Case Studies 6.1 Setup 

We conducted an expert-oriented qualitative case study with two professional digital artists (Artist A and B) to evaluate PaintCopilot in realistic co-creative painting workflows. Artist A is a profes-sional animator and character designer with professional indus-try experience and over 20 years of painting practice, while Artist B is an artist with an architectural background and more than 5 years of experience in multi-medium painting practices. Both artists regularly use pressure-sensitive tablets and professional painting software in their daily practice. Each artist participated in a 30–45 minute free-form portrait painting session using PaintCopilot on a pressure-sensitive tablet. Before the session, we introduced the system’s core interaction mechanisms, including autoregressive continuation, history optimization, dynamic brush refinement, and lasso-based local editing. Artists were encouraged to freely exper-iment with these features while painting naturally in their own style and continuously provide feedback during interaction. Rather than evaluating reconstruction fidelity against a predefined target image, the study focused on whether PaintCopilot could function as a practical co-creative painting assistant during open-ended artistic workflows. Participants commented on controllability, stylistic con-sistency, composition preservation, and interaction fluidity, while also identifying limitations such as occasional semantic drift and reduced controllability during long autonomous continuations. 

6.2 Case Study1: Composition-Guided Collaboration 

Artist A approached PaintCopilot as a refinement-oriented painting assistant rather than a fully autonomous generation system. The artist first established the global composition manually, including facial proportions, lighting structure, and large color regions, before gradually introducing AI assistance. During this stage, the artist experimented with multiple brush types provided by the system, including Hard Round brushes for compositional blocking and tex-tured Brush Tip modes for softer painterly transitions. The artist commented that the pressure-sensitive brush dynamics felt similar to professional digital painting workflows. After the composition became stable, the artist began alternat-ing between manual painting and short PaintCopilot continuation bursts. Rather than delegating the entire canvas to the AI, the artist repeatedly paused to inspect whether generated strokes preserved facial orientation, lighting consistency, and brush rhythm. The artist observed that PaintCopilot was particularly effective at extending local texture and shading in regions such as hair, clothing, and soft shadow transitions .Lasso-based local editing became an important interaction mech-anism during refinement. The artist constrained continuation to semantically flexible regions while preserving manual control over identity-sensitive facial structures. The artist remarked that this 

regional control substantially increased trust in the system because it allowed AI assistance without risking major compositional er-rors. The artist also experimented with Dynamic Brush refinement and history optimization, observing that these features improved local consistency and global coherence while occasionally reducing expressive painterly irregularities when applied excessively. Overall, Artist A maintained high-level compositional control throughout the session while selectively delegating localized re-finement tasks to PaintCopilot. Rather than replacing the artist’s workflow, the system primarily functioned as a cooperative refine-ment assistant that reinforced stylistic continuity and accelerated iterative painting refinement.

7 Discussion and Future Work 

PaintCopilot explores a different formulation of neural painting by modeling painting as an open-ended sequential creative process rather than a target-image reconstruction problem. Instead of op-timizing strokes toward a predefined reference image, the system predicts future brushstrokes from evolving canvas states and prior artistic behavior, enabling more interactive and co-creative painting workflows. Our case studies suggest that artists primarily use PaintCopilot as a collaborative assistant for refinement, exploration, and stylis-tic continuation rather than as a fully autonomous generator. In practice, artists continuously alternate between manual painting and AI-assisted continuation, forming an iterative feedback loop between human and AI decisions. Despite promising results, several limitations remain. Long au-toregressive continuations may accumulate semantic drift, partic-ularly in structurally sensitive facial regions. In addition, our cur-rent experiments focus mainly on portrait painting, and extending the framework to broader artistic domains may require more di-verse datasets and stronger compositional reasoning. Future work could further investigate hierarchical planning, long-range semantic consistency, and multimodal interaction such as language-guided artistic intent or sketch-based conditioning. More broadly, we believe PaintCopilot represents a step toward temporally grounded co-creative AI systems that participate in evolving artistic workflows rather than simply generating static images. 

8 Conclusion 

We presented PaintCopilot, a co-creative neural painting system that introduces autonomous painting continuation, modeling paint-ing as an open-ended autoregressive artistic behavior conditioned on evolving canvas states and prior brushstroke history, without requiring a predefined target image. The framework proposes three complementary models: a ViT-based Target Predictor that infers artist intent from partial canvas observations, an Autoregressive Next Stroke Predictor that gener-ates temporally coherent brushstrokes via flow matching, and a VAE-based Region Sampler that synthesizes semantically localized stroke sequences on demand. Built on three differentiable brush representations, the system supports four interactive workflows enabling seamless human-AI collaboration throughout the painting process. Through case studies with professional digital artists, we demonstrated that PaintCopilot enables fluid co-creative painting workflows in which artists and AI continuously alternate control. We believe this work opens new directions for neural creative tools that model artistic processes and human-AI collaboration rather than only static image generation.
